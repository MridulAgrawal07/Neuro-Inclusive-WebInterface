Scientists Discover New Method for Ocean Cleanup

Published April 2, 2026 · 5 min read

Researchers at the Institute of Marine Sciences have developed an innovative passive collection system that uses ocean currents to aggregate microplastic particles with significantly higher efficiency than previous approaches. The team published their findings in the journal Nature Environmental Solutions this week.

The device, roughly the size of a shipping container, can process approximately 50,000 litres of seawater per hour while consuming no external power. It relies on a series of ultra-fine hydrophilic mesh screens whose pore geometry was optimised using computational fluid dynamics modelling over three years of simulation.

Lead researcher Dr Amara Osei said the breakthrough came from rethinking assumptions about laminar flow at the micro scale. "We had to abandon the conventional wisdom that mesh density and flow rate are fundamentally at odds," she explained in a press briefing.

Field Trials

Initial field trials in the North Pacific Gyre — home to the Great Pacific Garbage Patch — will begin in June. The team hopes to deploy a fleet of 200 units within 18 months, pending funding from the UN Environment Programme.
